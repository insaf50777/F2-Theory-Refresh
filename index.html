WACC

2 main sources of finance for a listed company: EQUITY & DEBT

Equity: Ordinary share capital, reserves and irredeemable preference shares

Debt: Bank loans, redeemable preference shares, debentures, bonds, lease

liabilities, etc.

Equity holders expect a dividend - Cost of equity (calculated using the dividend

valuation model)

Debt providers expect an interest - Cost of debt

Since interest has a tax benefit, the post-tax cost of debt has to be considered.

E.g., if cost of debt is 8% and the tax rate is 30%, the post-tax cost of debt would be

8% * (1 - 0.3) = 8%*0.7 = 5.6%

Cost of equity is ALWAYS higher than cost of debt….

This is since ordinary shareholders accept a higher risk compared to debt providers.

Another reason for this is the tax benefit enjoyed by debt.

Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC)

Since most companies use multiple sources of finance with different costs of capital

attached to each source, the WACC calculates an average cost of capital using all

the sources. This will be calculated based on the market values of each source.

Example

Dividend paid this year = 40c
Expected dividend growth rate = 4%
Current market price per share = $6
Number of ordinary shares = 1,000,000 with a nominal value of $2 each
Page 2
Pre-tax cost of debt = 7.5%
Tax rate = 30%
Market value of debt = $2,700,000

Calculate the WACC

Cost of equity = [0.4*1.04] / 6 + 0.04 = 0.416/6 + 0.04 = 10.9%

Post-tax cost of debt = 7.5%*70% = 5.25%
Market value of equity = 1,000,000*6 = 6,000,000
Market value of debt = 2,700,000
Total market value = 8,700,000
WACC = 0.109*(6,000,000/8,700,000) + 0.0525*(2,700,000/8,700,000)

= 9.1%

If the business undertakes a project with a higher business risk, the WACC used

should be higher (i.e., a risk premium should be added). For example, if WACC is

12%, we can add a risk premium of, say, 2% and use 14% as the cost of capital.

When can the current WACC be used as a discount rate?

The current WACC is often used as a discount rate when using net present value or internal rate

of return calculations. However, this is only appropriate if the following conditions are met:

1 The capital structure is constant. If the capital structure changes, the weightings in the WACC

will also change.

2 The new investment does not carry a different business risk profile to the existing entity's

operations.

3 The new investment is marginal to the entity. If we are only looking at a small investment then

we would not expect any of cost of equity, cost of debt or the WACC to change materially. If the

investment is substantial, it will usually cause these values to change.

Example

Current capital structure:

Equity = $20m (50%)
Debt = $20m (50%)

Cost of equity = 14%

Post-tax cost of debt = 8%
Current WACC = 0.14*20/40 + 0.08*20/40 = 11%

Scenario 1

The business plans to invest in a new project with an investment of $10m. The project is financed

by 50% equity and 50% debt. What is the COC used to appraise this project?

We can use the current WACC of 11% since the capital structure will not change.

Scenario 2

The business plans to invest in a new project with an investment of $10m. The project is financed

by 100% equity. What is the COC used to appraise this project?

We should recalculate the WACC since the capital structure has changed.

WACC = 0.14*30/50 + 0.08*20/50 = 11.6%

Scenario 3

The business plans to invest in a new project with an investment of $1m. The project is financed

by 100% equity. What is the COC used to appraise this project?

We can use the current WACC of 11% since this new investment is marginal to the value to the

entity (i.e., the current value of the business is $40m but this investment is only $1m and hence

a very small investment).

  • If new shares are issued - WACC will increase since cost of equity is higher than cost of debt
  • If new debt is raised - WACC will decrease

Consignment inventory

Scenario 1

HP purchases inventory from a manufacturer named ABC Ltd where the agreement

states that HP will bear the loss of any unsold goods.

In this case, HP will record the purchase and inventory value on the day the

manufacturer transfers the items. ABC Ltd will account for the revenue on the day

the items are transferred to HP.

Scenario 2

HP purchases inventory from a manufacturer named DEF Ltd where the agreement

states that DEF Ltd will bear the loss of any unsold goods.

In this case, HP will not pass any entries upon receiving the goods and the inventory

will still be recorded in the books of DEF Ltd. Upon a sale being made to the

customer, HP will record the revenue and include the item under cost of sales and

at this point, DEF Ltd can derecognize the inventory and record revenue.

Associates:

Equity accounting

  • Goodwill is not calculated
  • Assets, liabilities, incomes and expenses are NOT consolidated line by line
  • In the CSOFP = A single line under non-current assets stating “Investment in

associate”

  • In the CSOPL = A single line below operating profit stating “Share of profit

from associate”

  • Investment in associate in the SOFP = Original investment value + Parent’s %

of associate’s post acquisition reserves - Total impairment of the investment

  • Share of profit from associate = (Associate’s profit for the year*Parent’s %) -

Impairment of investment during the current year

  • Intra-group transactions are NOT eliminated except parent’s share of any

provision for unrealized profit in inventory

Interpretation of financial statements (Ratio analysis)

Profitability ratios

Gross Profit margin = Gross profit / Revenue
  • This will be affected by the selling price, the production (or purchasing) cost

and the mix between products.

  • If sales revenue increases, this will not directly affect GP margin. Because

sales revenue and cost of sales will increase proportionately.

Operating Profit margin = Operating profit / Revenue
  • Operating profit = Gross profit - Operational expenses (excluding finance

cost and tax)

  • Will be affected by the same factors affecting GP margin
  • In addition to that, control over administration and distribution costs will

affect OP margin

Net Profit margin = Net profit / Revenue
  • Will be affected by the same factors affecting OP margin
  • In addition to that, finance cost and tax will also affect net profit
Return on capital employed = Operating profit / Capital employed
  • Capital employed = Equity + Long-term borrowing
  • Capital employed is also referred to as Net Assets (Total assets - Current

liabilities)

  • Will be affected by the same factors affecting OP margin
  • One of the most important ratios checked by investors
  • A share issue or a borrowing would have an immediate impact of reducing

ROCE. However, if the money is invested in a profitable manner, future

ROCE will improve.

Page 12
Return on equity = Profit for the year / Equity
  • Money raised through borrowings will not affect the denominator but can

improve the ROE if the money generates profit quickly.

Asset turnover ratio = Revenue / Capital employed
  • Assesses the revenue generating ability of the assets invested
Operating profit margin * Asset turnover ratio = ROCE

Liquidity ratios

Current ratio = Current assets / Current liabilities
  • Generally, a ratio of 2:1 is preferred, although the ideal current ratio

depends on the industry.

  • If the figure is too high, this is an indication that the business is not making

best use of the assets such as cash and also reflects a high risk due to high

inventory and high receivables.

Quick ratio (acid rest ratio) = (Current assets - Inventory) / Current liabilities

  • Removes the least liquid asset which is inventory and the numerator is

referred to as “quick assets”

  • Generally, a ratio of 1:1 is preferred, although the ideal quick ratio depends

on the industry.

Working capital ratios

Inventory days (inventory turnover period) = Inventory / Cost of sales * 365

  • Measures how long inventory remains in the business before being sold.
  • Lower the better.

Receivables days (receivables collection period) = Receivables / Revenue * 365

  • Measures how long it takes to collect money from credit customers.
  • Lower the better.
Page 13

Payables days (payables payment period) = Payables / Cost of sales * 365

  • Measures how long the business takes to settle its creditors
  • Higher the better. However, if payments are dragged for long, it will affect

the relationship with suppliers.

Working capital cycle = Inventory days + Receivables days - Payables days
  • Shows how long it takes for inventory to be converted to cash
  • Lower the better
  • A higher WCC will increase the finance cost since money will have to be

borrowed to settle suppliers

Capital structure (risk) ratios

Gearing ratio = Debt capital / (Debt capital + Equity capital)
  • A gearing ratio of above 50% is generally considered to be risky although it

depends on the industry.

Debt to equity ratio = Debt capital / Equity capital
  • A ratio of above 100% is generally considered to be risky although it

depends on the industry.

Interest cover = Operating profit / Finance cost
  • Measures how many times the profit can cover interest
  • Higher the better
Page 14

Debt vs Equity

  • Equity does not have to be repaid. Need not pay a guaranteed annual return.
  • However, debt is repayable along with an annual interest. This makes debt

riskier for the business.

  • Issuing equity instead of debt will lower the gearing ratio.
  • Raising debt will increase the gearing ratio which would make the company

appear risky in the eyes of investors.

  • Debt may have covenants attached to it. Covenants are restrictive conditions

imposed by the debt provider upon providing debt such as dividend payment

limits, acceptable profit margins and other ratios, further borrowings, etc.

  • When raising debt through sources such as bank loans, collateral assets

would have to be placed. This would make it difficult to sell those assets.

  • A share issue is time consuming and costlier than raising debt. When a share

issue is made, it will involve issuing a prospectus, underwriting charges and

other administrative cost.

  • If capital is raised through equity, this will increase the number of shares in

issue and hence the EPS will reduce. Shareholders may be unhappy if EPS

reduces.

  • Raising capital through shares will increase the equity portion of the capital

structure. Since cost of equity is always higher than cost of debt, this will

increase the WACC.

  • If a project takes some time to generate positive cash flows and if the project

is a long term one, it is advisable to go for equity.

  • For a medium-term project (say 3 to 5 years), it is advisable to go for debt.

Rights issue

➢ Faster and less costly compared to a public issue.

➢ However, the amount received will be lower than a public issue since the share

price is given at a discounted rate. Furthermore, some of the existing

shareholders may not subscribe to the rights issue and this can reduce the

proceeds.

Page 15

➢ It is generally accepted a rights issue should raise at least 20% or more of the

existing capital value. If the amount of capital to be raised is lower than this, it

is advisable to go for debt financing.

IAS 37

Liability:

Obligation with a certain amount and certain timing

Provision:

Uncertain amount and uncertain timing

Conditions to be satisfied:

1. Present obligation due to a past event

2. Probable outflow of economic benefits

3. Reliable estimate can be made

Probable = Probability is more than 50%
Possible = Probability is less than 50%

Example:

Year end is on 31/Dec/2022.

A customer slipped and fell in the premises on 12/Dec/2022 and now taking legal

action for negligence. The legal case will go on to 15/Jan/2023. Lawyers say that

there is a 70% chance we will lose the case and have to pay a compensation of

$10,000.

On 31/Dec/2022:

  • Legal obligation due to a past event
  • Probable since it is 70%
  • There is a reliable estimate

Accounting treatment - Include a provision under current liabilities in the SOFP of

$7,000 and include a corresponding expense in the SOPL of $7,000

Obligations:

  • Legal obligation - E.g., a legal case
  • Constructive obligation - E.g., An exchange or return policy publicised by the

company (although it was not legally necessary)

There is a legal obligation since a legislation has been passed. When the company

If the rectification has to be done after a few years, then the provision should be

Answer C

The provision should be created for the entire future estimate. Every year, the

provision should be recalculated and any increase/decrease should be adjusted.

Expected losses:

There is no obligation to continue the business if it is expecting a loss. Therefore,

Restructuring:

The provision for restructuring cannot include the cost of redeploying and training

the affected staff. Because there is no obligation to redeploy or train staff

members. The provision should include the redundancy package cost and legal

cost.

A provision cannot be created since the closure is not announced/communicated

to the affected employees.

Contingent liability:

  • Probability less than 50%, or
  • Probability more than 50% but reliable estimate cannot be made

  • Separately identifiable
  • Cost must be measured reliably
  • Probable future economic benefits

Netflix acquires a movie licence valid for 10 years at a cost of $20 million.

Every year, amortization expense = 20/10 = $2m

IAS 33 - Earnings per share

EPS is the only accounting ratio governed by an accounting standard.

EPS = Earnings / Weighted average number of shares
Earnings = Profit after tax - Irredeemable preference share dividend

The business has to always disclose this year’s EPS and last year’s EPS (comparative

EPS).

Example

The profit for the year is $2,500,000. The irredeemable preference share dividend

is $300,000. The company had 1,000,000 shares at the start of the year and issued

a further 600,000 shares on 01/Oct. The year end is on 31/Dec. Calculate the EPS.

Earnings = 2,500,000 - 300,000 = 2,200,000
Weighted average number of shares = 1,000,000 + (600,000 * 3/12) = 1,150,000
EPS = 2,200,000/1,150,000 = 191c per share of $1.91 per share
Page 6

Bonus issue

This is where shares are issued to existing shareholders free of charge. This is

generally issued as an alternative to a dividend payment.

If a bonus issue is made, it is assumed that these shares were in existence from the

commencement of the business. Last year’s EPS should be adjusted with the bonus

shares (and therefore it will reduce).

Rights issue

A rights issue is when shares are issued only to existing shareholders at a discount

to the market price.

When a rights issue is made, a component of this is a regular share issue and a

component is a bonus issue. For the bonus issue component, we have to adjust last

year’s comparative EPS (it will reduce). The regular component should be

accounted the normal way (time-apportion when calculating the weighted average

number of shares).

Example

The current share price is $5 and the company makes a rights issue of 200,000

shares for $4. This means that 1/5 or 20% of the shares are similar to a bonus issue.

Therefore, this is like issuing 200,000*20% = 40,000 shares free (i.e., bonus issue).

These 40,000 bonus shares will be added to last year’s and this year’s denominator

and hence will reduce last year’s and current year’s EPS.

Diluted earnings per share (DEPS)

DEPS represents the possible impact on the future EPS due to instruments such as

convertible debt and share options.

A convertible debt is a loan issued by the company where the holder is allowed to

convert the loan to shares upon maturity if he/she wishes to do so.

Page 7

A share option is an option for an individual to purchase shares in the company at

a pre-agreed price at a particular point of time.

A convertible bond could possibly increase the number of shares in future and

therefore dilute the EPS. Similarly, share options could also increase the number of

shares in future and therefore dilute the EPS. The DEPS will show the EPS IF the

convertible bond is converted and share options are exercised.

Another point we need to remember is that if a convertible bond is converted to

shares, this would reduce the finance cost (interest expense) since finance cost only

applies for debt and not for equity.

When calculating the number of shares from options, we have to consider the

following formula:

No. of options × ( [FV - EP] / FV)

FV = Fair value (market price of share)
EP = Exercise price

This formula is similar to how we calculated bonus shares in the rights issue note

above.

Example

The company has issued 300,000 share options where shares can be purchased for

a discounted price of $5 each. The current share price is $8.

Number of options to consider DEPS = 300,000 * 3/8 = 112,500
Page 8

Example

The earnings of the company is $400,000 and the number of shares amounts to

300,000. It has issued a convertible bond for $100,000 with an interest of 8% and

the bond holders have the option of converting this to 100,000 shares in 03 years’

time. The company has also issued share options for 50,000 shares. The market

price of an ordinary share was $5 and the exercise price for the shares under option

was $3. Calculate the EPS and the DEPS.

EPS = 400,000/300,000 = $1.33 per share
If convertible bond is converted to shares, there is an interest saving = 100,000*8%

= 8,000

Number of options = 50,000 * 2/5 = 20,000
DEPS = (Earnings + Notional earnings) / (Weighted number of shares + Notional

shares)

= (400,000 + 8,000) / (300,000 + 100,000 + 20,000)

= 408,000 / 420,000 = $0.97 per share

Foreign Subsidiaries

Translation of SOPL & OCI

•

Income and expenses at the average rate for the year

Translation of SOFP

•

Assets and liabilities at the closing rate

•

Goodwill at the closing rate

Exchange gains / losses

  • Exchange differences on Net Assets arise as the net assets are initially translated at

the opening rate, the closing net assets are translated at the closing rate and net

assets change (movement on net assets) is translated at average rate in OCI.

  • Exchange difference on goodwill arises as the goodwill b/f is translated at the

opening rate, the goodwill at the end of the period is translated at the closing rate

and any impairments of goodwill has to translated at the average rate in SOPL.

  • Sum of the above two differences is recognized within OCI. Exchange difference is

always split between parent and NCI based on the shareholding percentage.

  • Exchange difference on Goodwill may depend upon the way of measuring NCI:

1. If NCI is measured based on the proportion of net assets, parent has to bear up

the total goodwill difference.

2. If NCI is measured based on fair value method, goodwill difference is split

between the parent and NCI.

IAS 21 - Effect of Changes in Foreign

Exchange Rates

•

Presentation Currency: The currency in which the financial statements are prepared

•

Functional Currency: The currency of the primary economic environment in which the entity

operates. In most cases it’s the local currency.

•

Foreign Currency: Any currency other than the functional currency

•

Spot Exchange Rate: Actual exchange rate for a given day

•

Closing Rate: Exchange rate at the reporting date (year-end)

How to determine your functional currency?

1. The currency that mainly influences selling price for goods and services

2. The currency of the country whose competitive forces (demand and supply) and regulations mainly

determine the selling price of goods and services

3. The currency that mainly influences labour, material and other costs of providing goods and services

4. The currency in which funding from debt and equity is generated

5. The currency in which receipts from operating activities are usually retained

Initial Recognition

The transaction will be initially recorded using the spot exchange rate of the date of the transaction.

Subsequent Measurement - Settled transactions

When a cash settlement occurs, the settlement value must be measured using the spot exchange rate at

the date of the settlement. Any exchange gain or loss will be transferred to the statement of profit &

loss.

Subsequent Measurement - Unsettled transactions

Unsettled transactions are any foreign currency assets or liabilities remaining in the statement of

financial position at the year end. The accounting treatment will depend whether they are classified as

monetary or non-monetary.

Monetary items - Currency held and assets or liabilities to be received or paid in a foreign currency.

Page 2

E.g. cash, receivables, payables, loans

Accounting treatment: Translate using the closing rate. Any exchange gain or loss will be transferred to

the statement of profit & loss.

Non-Monetary items - Other items in the statement of financial position

E.g. Non-Current Assets, Inventory

Accounting treatment: Do not translate.

IAS 32 & IFRS 9 - Financial Instruments

IAS 32 deals with the classification of financial instruments and their presentation in financial

statements. A financial instrument is 'any contract that gives rise to a financial asset of one entity and a

financial liability or equity instrument of another entity simultaneously'.

As per IAS 32, 'a financial asset is any asset that is cash, an equity instrument of another entity, a

contractual right to receive cash or another financial asset from another entity, a contractual right to

exchange financial instruments with another entity under conditions that are potentially favourable'. Ex:

Investments in ordinary shares, Investments in debt instruments like debentures/loan stock/loan

notes/bonds etc.

A financial liability is ‘any liability that is a contractual obligation to deliver cash or another financial

asset to another entity to exchange financial instruments with another entity under conditions that are

from another entity, trade payables, and loans from financial institutions.

An equity instrument is 'any contract that evidences a residual interest in the assets of an entity after

deducting all of its liabilities'. Ex: an issue of ordinary shares.

Financial Derivatives

According to IFRS 9 Financial instruments, a derivative is a financial instrument that derives its value

from the value of an underlying asset, price, rate or index.

  • Underlying items include equities, bonds, commodities, interest rates, exchange rates and stock

market and other indices.

  • Derivative financial instruments include futures, options, forward contracts, interest rate and currency

swaps.

Characteristics of a derivative

A derivative has all of the following characteristics:

  • Its value changes in response to changes in the underlying item
  • It requires little or no initial investment
  • It is settled at a future date.

Recognition and measurement

All derivatives are accounted as fair value through profit or loss (FVPL). A derivatives gains (or losses)

fluctuate as the underlying item's price moves. On the date of initial recognition, no price movements

would have occurred so fair value is typically nil (as long as there is no cost of entering the derivative). At

each reporting date, the derivative is restated to fair value and recorded as a financial asset or financial

IFRS 15- Revenue from contracts with

customers

IFRS 15 defines revenue as ‘Income arising in the course of an entity’s ordinary activities. Revenue does

not include:

  • Proceeds from sale of non-current assets
  • Sales tax and other similar taxes
  • Other amounts collected on behalf of others. For example, in an agency relationship, the agent would

only recognise commission.

The five step process to revenue recognition can be fulfilled through the acronym (COPAR):
  • Contract
  • Obligations
  • Price
  • Allocate price
  • Recognise revenue.

Step 1 (CONTRACT) -Identify the contract

IFRS 15 Revenue from contracts with customers says that a contract is an agreement between two or

more parties that creates rights and obligations. A contract does not need to be written. An entity can

only account for revenue if the contract meets the following criteria:
  • The parties to the contract have approved and are committed to fulfilling the contract
  • Each party’s rights can be identified
  • The payment terms can be identified
  • The contract has commercial substance, and
  • It is probable that the entity will be paid.
Step 2: (OBLIGATIONS)-Identifying the separate performance obligations within a contract
Page 2

Hashan Bellanthuda

F2

Performance obligations are promises to transfer distinct goods or services to a customer. Some

contracts contain more than one performance obligation. For example:
  • An entity may enter into a contract with a customer to sell a car, which includes one year’s free

servicing and maintenance

  • An entity might enter into a contract with a customer to provide a course of 5 lectures, as well as to

provide a textbook on the first day of the course.

The distinct performance obligations within a contract must be identified. An entity must decide if the

nature of a performance obligation is:

  • To provide the specified goods or services itself (i.e. it is the principal), or
  • To arrange for another party to provide the goods or service (i.e. it is an agent).

If an entity is an agent, then revenue is recognised based on the fee or commission to which it is

entitled.

Step 3 (PRICE): Determining the transaction price

In accordance to IFRS 15 Revenue from contracts with customers, the transaction price is the amount of

consideration an entity expects in exchange for satisfying a performance obligation. When determining

the transaction price, the following must be considered:

a) Variable consideration - If a contract includes variable consideration (for example, a bonus

based on delivery of the contract), it shall be included within ‘the transaction price if it is highly

probable that a significant reversal in the amount of cumulative revenue will not occur when the

uncertainty is resolved’.

b) Significant financing components- Financing In determining the transaction price, an entity must

consider if the timing of payments provides the customer or the entity with a significant

financing benefit. The existence of a financing element to the sale can be indicated by:
  • A difference between the amount paid and the cash selling price
  • An extended time period between the transfer of the goods or service and the payment date.

If there is a significant financing component, then the consideration receivable needs to be

discounted to present value using the rate at which the customer would borrow.

c) Non-cash consideration- Customers do not always pay using cash or credit. The customer may

pay using shares in their entity, share options or using other assets. Any non-cash consideration

should be valued at fair value.

d) Consideration payable to a customer- Sometimes amounts are paid to customers as part of the

contract. These payments are often an incentive to encourage completion of the sale. If

consideration is paid to a customer in exchange for a distinct good or service, then it is

essentially a purchase transaction and should be accounted for in the same way as other

Page 3

Hashan Bellanthuda

F2

purchases from suppliers. For example, suppliers may sell their produce to supermarkets but

may also have to pay the supermarket for shelf space. If the consideration paid to a customer is

not in exchange for a distinct good or service, an entity should account for it as a reduction of

the transaction price. For example, a car manufacturer sells their newest model electric cars to

car dealerships but has to pay the dealer to install chargers at the dealer’s premises.

Step 4: Allocate the transaction price -The total transaction price should be allocated to each

performance obligation in proportion to stand-alone selling prices. The best evidence of a stand-alone

selling price is the observable selling price of a good or service sold separately. If a stand-alone selling

price is not directly observable, then the entity estimates the stand-alone selling price.

In relation to a bundled sale, any discount should generally be allocated across each component in the

transaction. The entire discount should only be allocated to a specific individual component of the

transaction if that component is regularly sold separately at a discount.

Step 5: Recognise revenue

Revenue is recognised 'when (or as) the entity satisfies a performance obligation by transferring a

promised good or service to a customer.’ At the start of a contract, for each performance obligation

identified, an entity must determine whether it satisfies the performance obligation:

•

At a point in time- The performance obligation is satisfied at a point in time when a

customer obtains control of a promised asset. Control of an asset refers to the ability to

direct the use of, and obtain substantially all of, the remaining benefits from the asset.

Control includes the ability to prevent other entities from obtaining benefits from an

asset i.e. an entity can restrict the assets use. The following are indicators of the transfer

of control:

  • ‘The entity has a present right to payment for the asset
  • The customer has legal title to the asset
  • The entity has transferred physical possession of the asset
  • The customer has the significant risks and rewards of ownership of the asset
  • The customer has accepted the asset.’

•

Over time

Consignment inventory

Consignment inventory is inventory which:

  • is legally owned by one party
  • is held by another party, on terms which give the holder the right to sell the inventory in the normal

course of business or, at the holder’s option, to return it to the legal owner.

  • the dealer sells inventory onto a third party, or
  • the dealer’s right to return expires and the inventory is still not sold.

However, the inventory is physically held by the dealer.

Sale and repurchase agreements

A sale and repurchase agreement is where an entity sells an asset but retains a right to repurchase the

asset at some point in the future.

Sale and repurchase agreements are common in property developments and in maturing inventories

such as whisky or cheese. The asset has been ‘legally’ sold, but there is either a commitment or an

option to repurchase the asset at a later date.

The point in time at which to recognise revenue depends upon whether the control has transferred to

the customer.

Factors to consider when determining who has control of the asset:
  • Has the entity transferred the risks and benefits of the asset? e.g. can the entity still use the asset?

Does the entity bear costs associated with the asset?

  • Was the asset "sold" at a price different to market value?
  • Is the entity obliged to repurchase the asset?
  • If the entity has the option to repurchase the asset are they likely to exercise this option?
  • Is the sale is to a bank or financing company?
  • Is repurchase at a fixed price or market value?

Accounting for revenue recorded over time

'For each performance obligation satisfied over time, an entity shall recognise revenue over time by

measuring the progress towards complete satisfaction of that performance obligation' (IFRS 15, para

39). Methods of measuring progress include:

  • Output methods (such as surveys of performance or time elapsed as a proportion of total contract

price/time)

  • Input methods (such as costs incurred to date as a proportion of total expected costs). As a result,

revenue will be recognised based on the amount of progress made compared to the total price.

Contract costs

IFRS 15 says that the following costs must be capitalised:

  • The incremental costs of obtaining a contract
  • The costs of fulfilling a contract if they do not fall within the scope of another standard (such as IAS 2

Inventories) and the entity expects them to be recovered.

The capitalised costs will be amortised as revenue is recognised. This means that they will be expensed

to cost of sales as the contract progresses. These will be matched to revenue based on either the input

or output method of measuring progress. Cost of sales will be measured as % progress made × total

costs.

IFRS 16- Leases

An entity may need a particular asset for its operations but may not have the cash available to purchase

the asset outright. As a result, the entity may enter a lease agreement whereby the entity gets to use

the asset on a day to day basis but pays for the asset via periodic rental payments.

Lessee accounting

The lessee is the entity using the asset on a day-to-day basis. It has the right-of-use of the asset. The

lessee will be paying rentals.

Basic principle

At the commencement of the lease, the lessee should recognise:

  • A lease liability, and
  • A right-of-use asset. As ever, the accounting treatment considers the initial measurement and the

subsequent treatment of a lease.

Initial measurement

Lease liability

Per IFRS 16 Leases, the lease liability is initially measured at the present value of the lease payments that

have not yet been paid.

Lease payments should include the following:

  • Fixed payments over the lease term.
  • Amounts expected to be payable under residual value guarantees. A residual value guarantee is

provided by the lessee and states that the underlying asset at the end of the lease term will not be

worth less than a specified amount. This reduces the risk of damage and unauthorized usage of the

asset.

  • Options to purchase the asset that are reasonably certain to be exercised.
  • Termination penalties, if the lease term reflects the expectation that these will be incurred.

The discount rate should be the rate implicit in the lease. If the implicit rate of the lease cannot be

determined, then the entity should use its incremental borrowing rate (the rate at which it could borrow

funds to purchase a similar asset).

The right-of-use asset

The right-of-use asset is initially recognised at cost.

Per IFRS 16 Leases, the initial cost of the right-of-use asset comprises:
Page 2

Hashan Bellanthuda

F2

  • The amount of the initial measurement of the lease liability (see above)
  • Lease payments made at or before the commencement date
  • Any initial direct costs
  • The estimated costs of removing or dismantling the underlying asset, as per the conditions of the

lease.

The lease term

The lease term is the length of time that the lessee has the right-of-use of an asset. To calculate the

initial value of the liability and right-of-use asset, the lessee must consider the length of the lease term.

As per IFRS 16 Leases, the lease term comprises:

  • Non-cancellable periods
  • Periods covered by an option to extend the lease, if they are reasonably certain to be exercised
  • Periods covered by an option to terminate the lease, if these are reasonably certain not to be

exercised.

Subsequent measurement

Lease liability

The carrying amount of the lease liability is increased by the interest charge. Interest is also recorded in

the statement of profit or loss:

•

Dr Finance costs (SPL) X

•

Cr Lease liability (SFP) X
The carrying amount of the lease liability is reduced by cash repayments:

•

Dr Lease liability X

•

Cr Cash X

The treatment of the lease liability is in line with the treatment of financial liabilities measured at

amortised cost as per IFRS 9 Financial instruments. The layout of the lease table will depend on whether

payments are made at the end of the year (in arrears) or at the start of the year (in advance).

Hashan Bellanthuda

F2

Short-life and low value assets

If the lease is short-term (less than 12 months at the inception date) or of a low value then a simplified

treatment is allowed. In these cases, the lessee can choose to recognise the lease payments in profit or

loss on a straight line basis. No lease liability or right-of-use asset would be recognised.

IFRS 16 Leases does not specify a particular monetary amount below which an asset would be

considered ‘low value’.

The standard gives the following examples of low value assets:

  • Tablets
  • Small personal computers
  • Telephones
  • Small items of furniture.

The assessment of whether an asset qualifies as having a ‘low value’ must be made based on its value

when new. Therefore, a car would not qualify as a low value asset, even if it was very old at the

commencement of the lease.

Lessor accounting

The lessor is the 'entity that provides the right to use an underlying asset in exchange for consideration.'

The lessor provides the asset to the lessee and receives payments. A lessor must classify its leases as

finance or operating leases.

Indications of a finance lease

To determine whether a lease is a finance or operating lease, the substance of the lease agreement

should be considered. According to IFRS 16 Leases, a lease is probably a finance lease if one or more of

the following apply:

Page 4

Hashan Bellanthuda

F2

  • Ownership is transferred to the lessee at the end of the lease.
  • The lessee has the option to purchase the asset for a price substantially below the fair value of the

asset and it is reasonably certain the option will be exercised.

  • The lease term is for the major part of the asset's useful life.
  • The present value of the minimum lease payments amounts to substantially all of the fair value of the

asset.

  • The leased assets are of such a specialised nature that only the lessee can use them without major

modification.

  • The lessee bears losses arising from cancelling the lease.
  • Lessee has ability to continue the lease for a secondary period at a rate below market rent.

Accounting treatment for an operating lease

  • Lease receipts are shown as income in the statement of profit or loss on a straight line basis over the

term of the lease, unless another systematic basis is more appropriate.

  • Any difference between amounts charged and amounts paid will be recognised as prepayments or

accruals in the statement of financial position.

Accounting treatment for a finance lease

At the inception of a lease, lessors present assets held under a finance lease as a receivable.

The finance lease receivable is equal to the net investment of the lease.

This is calculated as the present value of:

  • Fixed payments
  • Residual value guarantees (amounts the lessee guarantees that the leased asset will be worth at the

end of the lease)

  • Unguaranteed residual values
  • Termination penalties.

The payments are discounted at the rate implicit in the lease.

The carrying amount of the lease receivable is:

  • increased by the finance income earned
  • decreased by the cash receipts.

•

If the intangible is with indefinite useful life, no amortizations are carried out. Impairment

Income Tax Expenses

Income tax expenses in SOPL covers 3 elements:

1. Current tax expense for the year

Estimated amount of tax payable on the taxable profits of the entity for the financial year.

Dr Income tax expense (SPL)
Cr Income tax liability (SFP - current liability)

2. Under / Over provisions

Income taxes for period are paid in the next financial year. The difference between the amount

accrued (in the previous year) and the amount settled (in the current year) is recorded in the current

year's income tax expense as an under or over provision relating to the prior period.

•

Under provision = Amount settled > Amount previously recognized (Increases the tax

expense)

•

Over provision = Amount settled < Amount previously recognized (Reduces the tax expense)

3. Deferred Tax

Deferred tax includes the estimated future tax consequences of transactions and events recognised

in the financial statements of the current and previous periods. (This doesn’t reflect the amount

payable to tax authorities).

There’s a difference in between the accounting profit and the taxable profit due to the timing

difference. There can be either temporary differences or permanent differences.

Temporary differences are differences between the carrying amount of an asset or liability in the

statement of financial position and its tax base

Accounting for Deferred taxes

1. Calculate the temporary difference at the year end.

2. Deferred tax balance = Temporary difference * tax rate
3. Deferred tax expense = Increase in deferred tax balance in the year.

Deferred tax for losses

A deferred tax asset is recognised on unutilised losses carried forward (as there will be a future tax

benefit when the losses are offset against future profits). But the assets can be recognized only if it is

probable that the future tax profits will be available against which the losses can be utilized.

Impact on Other Comprehensive Income statement

Any deferred tax charge/credit that relates to an item that has been recognised in other

comprehensive income should also be recognised in other comprehensive income.

Integrated Reporting

Integrated Reporting aims to communicate how an entity will create value over time and

identify the key drivers of its value. To do this requires relevant financial and non-financial

information.

Non-financial reporting provides an idea of how the business is managed, its future

prospects, the entity’s policy on the environment, Its attitude towards social. Responsibility,

etc.

Objectives for integrated reporting

  • Improve the quality of information available to providers of financial capital
  • Provide more cohesive and efficient approach to corporate reporting
  • Enhance the accountability and stewardship for the broad base of capitals
  • Support integrated thinking and decision making.

Categories of capital

1. Financial capital: Obtained through financing via debt, equity, grants, etc.

2. Manufactured capital: Manufactured physical objects that are utilized in the

production of goods or services. Ex:) Buildings, Machinery, water utilities, etc.

3. Intellectual capital: Intellectual property such as patents, copyrights, softwares,

rights, organization procedures and protocols.

4. Human capital: Competencies, capabilities and experience, motivation of the

employees. Enhancing of human capital through education and trainings is critical to

an organizations’ future.

5. Social and Relationship capital: Institutions and the relationships within and

between communities, groups of stakeholders and other networks, and the ability to

share information to enhance individual and collective well-being. Ex:) Intangibles

associated with brands, shared norms, relationships with stakeholders, etc.

6. Natural capital: All environmental resources and processes that support the

organization. Ex:) Biodiversity, land, forests, climate, carbon emissions, etc.

Weighted Average Cost of Capital

Weighted average cost of capital is the average cost of the company’s finance (equity, loan

notes, bank loans, and preference shares) weighted according to the proportion each element

bears to the total pool of funds.

WACC can be used to evaluate the company’s future projects if the following conditions

apply:

  • No change in financial risk (Gearing ratio is same)
  • No change in business risk in the new project
  • Perfect capital markets (True opportunity cost of the investment is represented by

cost of capital)

  • Small project - costs of capital calculated refer to the minimum required return of

marginal investors and therefore are only appropriate for the evaluation of marginal

changes in the company’s total investment.

  • Pooled funds - All funds are regarded as forming a pool out of which all projects are

financed.

Market Value of sources of finance

1. Ordinary shares - Ex-div price per share x number of shares in issue

2. Preference shares - Ex-div price per share x number of shares in issue

3. Debentures - Book value x market value / 100

4. Bank loans - Book value (market value does not exist)
